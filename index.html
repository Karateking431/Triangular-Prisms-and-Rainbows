Triangular Prism and How the Rainbow Gets Formed

Today we will be looking into trianguler prisms, Rainbows, How they get formed and the relation between them

A triangular prism is a three-dimensional shape with two triangular bases and three rectangular sides. The bases run parallel to each other, connected by three rectangular faces. Triangular prisms are commonly encountered in everyday objects like rooftops, tents, and certain types of packaging due to their stable and visually appealing geometry.

Rainbows are natural meteorological phenomena that result from the dispersion, reflection, and refraction of sunlight in water droplets in the Earth's atmosphere. When sunlight enters a raindrop, it is refracted, reflecting off the inside surface of the droplet and dispersing into its component colors due to the different wavelengths of light bending at different angles. This dispersion and reflection create the familiar arc of colors that we see in the sky after rain.

How does a rainbow form?

Rainbows form when sunlight enters raindrops, refracts, reflects inside, then disperses into colors due to varying wavelengths. The dispersed light exits, creating a circular arc of colors. Red is on the outer edge, violet on the inner. Double rainbows occur with light reflecting twice inside a raindrop, reversing the color order. This natural spectacle demonstrates the principles of refraction, reflection, and dispersion, showcasing the beauty of light interacting with water droplets in the atmosphere.

What is the relation between a trianguler prism and a Rainbow?

While rainbows and triangular prisms may seem unrelated at first glance, there is an interesting optical connection between the two based on the principles of light refraction and dispersion.

When light enters a triangular prism, it undergoes refraction and dispersion, splitting into its component colors due to the different wavelengths of light bending at different angles. This effect is known as dispersion, where the white light is separated into a spectrum of colors. Similarly, in the formation of a rainbow, sunlight enters raindrops and undergoes both reflection and refraction. The water droplets act as tiny prisms, dispersing the sunlight into its constituent colors and creating the arc of colors we see in the sky. So, in a way, the dispersion of light within a triangular prism and the dispersion of sunlight within raindrops that leads to the formation of a rainbow both involve the separation of white light into its various colors. The triangular prism serves as a man-made example of how light behaves when it encounters different mediums, similar to the natural phenomenon of a rainbow in the sky.
